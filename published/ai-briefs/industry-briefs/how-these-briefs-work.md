Every brief in this bundle follows the same shape. Once you know it, you can read any
brief in a couple of minutes and know exactly where to look for the part you need.

## What's in every brief

Each brief opens with a **source header**: the title of the piece, the author and their
affiliation, when it was published, and a link to the original. The link matters — a
brief is our read of the analyst's work, not a replacement for it. If the brief changes
how you think, go read the source.

Then six sections, always in this order:

- **The one-line takeaway** — what the analyst argues and why you'd care, in a sentence.
- **The argument** — the analyst's core thesis in plain language. Not their conclusion
  alone, but the chain of reasoning that gets them there.
- **The evidence behind it** — the data, surveys, or comparisons the analyst uses to
  support the argument, with the actual figures where they appear.
- **How much to trust it** — the honest read. Who wrote it, what stake they have in the
  answer, how strong the underlying data is, and where the argument stretches further
  than the evidence.
- **So what — for you** — what you'd do differently because of this. The reason you're
  here.
- **The fine print** — limits, scope, and where not to over-apply the result.

## How to read "How much to trust it"

This is the section to slow down on. An analyst can be sharp and still have a commercial
interest in the conclusion. We flag those things plainly so you can weigh the piece
yourself.

Treat it as a flag to slow down, not a final verdict. A piece from a vendor-sponsored
firm isn't worthless — it's worth a second look. The section gives you the questions a
careful reader would ask. You decide what the answer is worth.

## The defining rule on figures

An analyst piece is a primary source for its own figures. We cite the piece for numbers
that appear in it. If the piece names a third-party source for a figure, we trace it
there and cite the original. If we can't trace it, we say so. We note the author's stake
where it's relevant, but stake doesn't mean unsourced — it means read carefully.

## How briefs are grouped

Briefs are sorted into themes so you can follow the area you care about. A theme appears
on the site once it has its first brief, so the list fills in over time. The themes we're
working toward:

- **Market and adoption** — how fast AI is actually landing in enterprises, and what that means for buyers and vendors.
- **Strategy and investment** — where capital is moving and what the strategic bets look like.
- **Vendors and platforms** — the competitive landscape and what the major players are doing.
- **Workforce and skills** — what AI means for jobs, roles, and how people work.
- **Risk and governance** — liability, regulation, and the decisions organizations have to make.

## What a brief is not

A brief is a starting point, not a citation you can lean on. We summarize one analyst
piece and add our own plain-language read — we don't run independent research. The
figures in a brief are checked against the piece itself, but the piece can still be
wrong. When a decision rides on a number, read the original and check it for yourself.

Each brief reflects the piece as it stood when we wrote it. A later piece doesn't change
an old brief — it gets its own. So a brief is a snapshot, dated at the top, of one
argument at one moment.
