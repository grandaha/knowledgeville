## When to use this

When you need to name something — a project, product, article, event, or team — and want a range of real options to react to rather than staring at a blinking cursor.

## The prompt

```
You are a naming expert. I need a batch of name or title options for [WHAT YOU ARE NAMING].

Generate 10 options across these three styles:
- **Descriptive** (tells you exactly what it is)
- **Evocative** (creates a feeling or image)
- **Unexpected** (surprising, memorable, or counterintuitive)

For each name, add one sentence explaining the angle or association behind it.

Here's the context: [DESCRIBE THE THING — its purpose, the audience, the tone you're going for, and any words or themes to lean into or avoid].
```

## Before and after

**Before:** Naming an internal knowledge-sharing program at a mid-size company. Tone should feel human and approachable, not corporate. Avoid anything with "Academy" or "University."

**After (excerpt):**

> **Descriptive**
> 1. **What We Know** — Simple, direct; signals this is about sharing real expertise, not performance.
> 2. **The How-We-Do-It Series** — Practical and conversational; sounds like something a colleague would say.
>
> **Evocative**
> 3. **Open Desk** — Suggests accessibility and an open-door culture; implies you can always pull up a chair.
> 4. **The Thread** — Knowledge that connects people and runs through the whole organization.
>
> **Unexpected**
> 5. **Show Your Work** — Borrowed from math class; reframes sharing knowledge as something honest and unpolished.
> 6. **The Draft** — Implies ideas in progress, not finished decrees; lowers the stakes for contributors.

## Make it yours

- Ask for a second round: "Keep the style of #5 and give me 6 more in that direction."
- Add "make them all one word" or "keep them under four syllables" for naming constraints.
- Paste in names you've already rejected and ask it to avoid that territory.
- Ask for domain or tagline suggestions alongside the names if you're naming something public-facing.

## Watch out for

Before you fall in love with a name, do a quick search. A name can feel perfect and turn out to be a registered trademark, an existing product, or a domain that's been squatted. AI won't catch conflicts — that check is on you.
