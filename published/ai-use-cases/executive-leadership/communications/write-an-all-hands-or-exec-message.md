## When to use this

You have something to tell the whole company. A win, a change, a quarter wrapping up, a new direction. You know the key points. You just need them shaped into a message people will read and feel good about.

The hard part isn't the facts. It's the tone. The same news lands differently as a warm note or a flat memo.

Use this when you want a strong first draft to react to. You'll still write the final version. This gets you off the blank page fast.

## The prompt

```
You are helping a CEO write a company-wide message. Turn the points below into a clear, on-tone message to the whole team.

Tone I want: [WARM AND PERSONAL / CALM AND STEADY / DIRECT AND BRIEF / CELEBRATORY — pick one or describe it]

Key points to cover:
[LIST THE POINTS — what happened, what's changing, what people should do or feel. Bullets are fine.]

Context the team already knows:
[ANYTHING THEY'VE ALREADY HEARD, so you don't over-explain. Optional.]

Write it as:
1. An opening line that gets to the point — no throat-clearing.
2. The body — the points above, in an order that flows.
3. A close — what happens next, or what I want people to take away.

Rules:
- Use only what I gave you. Don't invent news, numbers, or promises. If something seems missing, list it under "Worth adding?" at the end instead of guessing.
- Plain, human language. No corporate filler, no hype, no "synergy."
- Sound like a person talking to their team, not a press release.
```

## Before and after

**Before — the blunt points you'd paste in:**

> - We hit our annual revenue goal a quarter early <!-- noev: worked example -->
> - Want to thank the team, especially sales and support
> - Reminder: we're not slowing down, next goal is bigger
> - Tone: warm but not over the top

**After — the kind of message it produces:**

> **We hit our number — a full quarter early.**
>
> I wanted you to hear this from me first. We just crossed our revenue goal for the year, with a quarter to spare. <!-- noev: worked example -->
>
> That's not a spreadsheet milestone. It's the result of a lot of hard, often invisible work. Sales closed it. Support kept every customer we won. And everyone behind them made that possible.
>
> So, thank you. Genuinely.
>
> Here's the honest part: we're not coasting into the holidays. Hitting this early means we get to aim higher next year, and I think that's the fun part. More on what that looks like soon.
>
> For now — take the win. You earned it.

## Make it yours

- **Shift the tone.** Swap the tone line and re-run. *"Make it calmer"* or *"more celebratory"* changes the whole feel.
- **Match how you actually talk.** Paste an old message of yours and add *"Write it in this voice."*
- **Set the length.** Add *"Keep it to four short paragraphs"* or *"give me a 30-second version too."*
- **Pick the channel.** *"This is going in Slack, not email — make it shorter and looser."*
- **Pressure-test it.** Add *"How might this be misread? Flag any line that could land wrong."*

## Watch out for

- **Read it aloud before you send.** A leader's words carry further than anyone's. If a line doesn't sound like you when you say it, rewrite it. The final wording is yours, not the tool's.
- **AI drifts into generic corporate tone.** It loves "excited to announce" and "incredible journey." Cut those. Your team can tell the difference between you and a template.
- **Make sure it fits the moment.** A warm, upbeat draft is wrong for hard news. Match the tone to what people are actually feeling, not to what reads nicely.
- **You own every word.** People will quote this back to you. The draft is a starting point — edit it until it sounds like you.
