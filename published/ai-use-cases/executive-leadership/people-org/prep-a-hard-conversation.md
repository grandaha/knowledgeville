## When to use this

You have a hard conversation coming. A performance issue, a role change, a hard no. You know roughly what you want to say. You also know it could go sideways fast.

Use this to rehearse before the real thing. You play yourself; the AI plays the other person, or coaches you. You try an opening, hear how it lands, and try again. Running it a few times helps more than getting one line perfect.

This is a back-and-forth. You say a line, the AI reacts, you adjust. Run it a few rounds. The goal is to walk in calmer and clearer, with the hard turns already rehearsed.

## A prompt to start

Open the loop with something like this, then keep going round after round:

```
I have a difficult conversation to prepare for. I'll keep names and identifying details out of this — treat the other person in general terms.

The situation: [ONE OR TWO SENTENCES, NO NAMES]
My goal for the conversation: [WHAT YOU WANT TO BE TRUE WHEN IT ENDS]
What I'm worried about: [THE REACTION OR TURN YOU DREAD]

Play the other person. Stay in character and react the way someone in that spot plausibly would. I'll give you my opening line. Push back, get defensive, or go quiet — whatever fits. After each round, drop character briefly and tell me what landed and what didn't.

Here's my opening:
[YOUR FIRST LINE]
```

You can also flip it. Ask the AI to coach instead of role-play: "Don't play them — critique my line and suggest a clearer one."

## Work through it step by step

1. **Name your goal first.** Before any wording, get clear on what you want true when it ends. Clarity for them? A behavior change? A clean exit? The goal shapes every line after it.
2. **Draft the opening out loud.** Say your first two sentences as you'd really say them, then type them in. The opening sets the tone for the whole conversation. Get it human, not robotic.
3. **Role-play the reactions.** Let the AI push back as the other person. They get defensive; you respond. They go quiet; you respond. Run the turns that scare you most.
4. **Tighten what didn't land.** When a line comes out cold or vague, fix it. Ask: "Say that more directly but still kind." Keep the version that sounds like you.
5. **Plan for the hard turn.** There's usually one moment that could derail it — tears, anger, a "this isn't fair." Rehearse that exact moment. Have a calm next line ready so you're not improvising under pressure.
6. **Do one last clean run.** Walk the whole conversation start to finish. If it still sounds like a script, loosen it until it sounds like you talking.

## What stays your call

The relationship, the empathy, and the decision are all yours. The AI can suggest words and how they might land. But it doesn't know the person you're talking to, and it isn't the one making the call.

A rehearsed line should still sound like you. If the AI hands you something polished but not your voice, change it. The other person will know the difference between a real conversation and a recited one. Use the prep to be ready, not to be scripted.

## Watch out for

- **Keep it anonymous.** Don't paste a real person's name, role, or identifying details into a chat tool you don't control. Rehearse in general terms — "a team member," not who. The situation matters for the prep; their identity doesn't.
- **Don't rehearse away the empathy.** It's easy to optimize a hard message into something efficient and cold. Stay warm. The point is to deliver hard news like a person, not to win the exchange.
- **A smooth script can sound fake.** If you memorize lines word for word, you'll sound like you're reading. Rehearse the shape and the hard turns, then trust yourself to talk in the room.
- **The AI's version of the other person is a guess.** It doesn't know this person. Use the role-play to practice staying steady, not to predict exactly what they'll say.
