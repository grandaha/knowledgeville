This page is the map. It shows how **Executive Leadership** is organized so you can go straight to the work in front of you — a board update due Friday, a budget review to prep, a hard conversation you've been putting off.

## Organized by the job a leader owns

The pages are grouped by **leadership function** — the kind of work, not your exact title. A CEO, CFO, and COO share most of these jobs; you'll dip into whichever one matches the task at hand. There's no order to work through. Jump to the function you need:

- **[Strategy & Planning](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/strategy-planning/index.md)** — set direction, scan the field, pressure-test the plan
- **[Finance & Performance](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/finance-performance/index.md)** — read the numbers and prep the reviews
- **[People & Org](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/people-org/index.md)** — org and hiring calls, and the conversations that come with them
- **[Board & Investor](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/board-investor/index.md)** — updates and the Q&A that follows
- **[Communications](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/communications/index.md)** — the messages a leader sends, routine and high-stakes
- **[Decisions & Meetings](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/decisions-meetings/index.md)** — make the call explicit and run the room

## Two kinds of page

Leadership work isn't all one shape, so the pages aren't either:

- **Recipes** hand you a ready-to-paste prompt for a concrete task — a board update, a one-page read of a finance deck, a decision memo. Copy it, drop in your details, run it.
- **Workflows** walk you through a judgment-heavy job — an org decision, a scenario stress-test, a crisis message — where the value is in the thinking, not a single prompt. They show where AI helps and where you stay in the driver's seat.

Every page is built around *doing the thing*, not reading about it.

## What stays yours

AI drafts fast, and it is sometimes confidently wrong. On leadership work the stakes are higher than a typo: a number in a board deck, the tone of a layoff message, the framing of a strategic bet. Treat every output as a strong first draft — check the facts that matter, and keep the final wording and the final call your own. The [glossary](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/glossary.md) explains the few terms used here.
