Most of an agent's day is words: a listing that has to sing, a follow-up that can't sound
canned, a script for a nervous seller, a plain answer to "what's a contingency?" AI is good
at first drafts of exactly this. These recipes hand you the prompt and show you the result,
so you spend your time editing instead of staring at a blank screen.

## How the recipes are grouped

Each recipe lives under the job you're doing, not the tool you're using:

- **[Listings & Marketing](/ai-use-cases/realtors/listings-and-marketing/index.md)** — the copy that sells a property and fills an open house.
- **[Prospecting & Outreach](/ai-use-cases/realtors/prospecting-and-outreach/index.md)** — the messages that start conversations with new sellers and your own sphere.
- **[Client Communication](/ai-use-cases/realtors/client-communication/index.md)** — the notes that keep a client informed and comfortable through a deal.

Two more groups are on the way as recipes are written: **Market & Advising** (neighborhood
and pricing explainers) and **Transactions & Admin** (showing notes, checklists, disclosure
explainers). A group shows up here once it has its first recipe.

## What every recipe looks like

The shape is the same each time, so you learn it once:

- **When to use this** — the moment the recipe fits.
- **The prompt** — paste-ready, with the blanks you fill in.
- **Before and after** — the raw input and what the AI turned it into.
- **Make it yours** — how to tune the voice and reuse it.
- **Watch out for** — only where a recipe touches a rule you can't get wrong.

## Where you keep the judgment

This is the part AI does not do for you. An agent stays on the hook for three things, every
time:

- **Fair housing.** AI will happily write phrases that steer or describe the *buyer* instead
  of the home. That's a legal risk. You read every listing and every ad with fair-housing
  eyes before it goes out.
- **Accuracy.** Square footage, school ratings, "walk to the train," what a clause means —
  the AI guesses when it doesn't know. You confirm the facts.
- **It isn't legal advice.** A plain-language explainer helps a client understand a term. It
  doesn't replace their attorney, and you shouldn't let it read that way.

The recipes flag these where they bite. But the rule holds even where a page stays quiet:
the AI drafts, and you own what you send.
