## When to use this

You've picked a neighborhood to farm and you want to get a letter or postcard in the door. You know the area and you've got a recent sale to point to. But sitting down to write it takes longer than it should. Use this to get a workable first draft, then tune it to sound like you.

## The prompt

Paste this into ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Fill in the brackets.

```text
You're helping me write a neighborhood farming letter. Write a warm,
professional letter I can mail to homeowners in a target neighborhood.

Details:
- Neighborhood name: [e.g. Maplewood Estates]
- Recent local sale or market note: [e.g. a home on Birch Lane closed at
  $485,000 last month — the highest sale on that street in two years]
- My angle or value I bring: [e.g. I've closed 7 homes in this zip code
  in the past year]
- Tone: [friendly / professional / conversational]
- Length: [short postcard blurb, about 80 words / full letter, about 200 words]

Rules:
- Address the reader as a homeowner in the neighborhood, not by any
  personal characteristic. Never reference who lives there or imply
  anything about the community's demographics.
- Use ONLY the market facts I provided. Do not invent prices, days on
  market, or any other statistics.
- Do NOT include any number I didn't supply. If a fact field is blank,
  leave it out entirely.
- End with a clear, low-pressure call to action — a free home valuation,
  a quick call, or an invitation to reach out with questions.
- Sign off with: [Your name], [Your brokerage], [Your phone/email]
```

## Make it yours

- **Use your actual recent sale.** The more specific the address and price, the more the letter reads as local knowledge, not a mail-merge. Neighbors notice a Birch Lane sale because they know that street.
- **Adjust the call to action.** Swap the free valuation for a market report, a neighborhood stats sheet, or a coffee invite — whatever fits your farm strategy.
- **Try a postcard version.** Set length to "short postcard blurb, about 80 words" and you get a tighter version for a smaller format. Run both and pick whichever fits the format.
- **Save the prompt.** Once the tone reads like you, keep it. Next mailing, you only swap the neighborhood name and the recent sale.

## Watch out for

**Any market number you share is yours to stand behind.** The AI uses only what you give it — so if you say "$485,000," that number goes in the letter. Make sure it's accurate before it hits the mail. Never fill in a sale price or days-on-market figure from memory; pull it from MLS data first. A wrong number in a farming letter can undercut your credibility with the exact homeowners you're prospecting. <!-- noev: illustrative sample figure in a demo -->

**Geographic targeting can raise fair-housing concerns.** Choosing which neighborhood to farm isn't automatically neutral. The protected classes include race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, and disability. Selecting a farm area that tracks any of those can look like steering, even if that wasn't your intent. Farm by geography and market activity: boundaries, turnover rate, price point. Not by who lives there.

**Solicitation rules vary by area.** Do-not-mail and do-not-contact rules differ by state and municipality. If you plan to follow up a mailed letter with a call or door-knock, check the applicable do-not-call rules for your area before you dial. When in doubt, your broker or a real estate attorney can point you to the right local guidance.
