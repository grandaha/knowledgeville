This page is the map. It shows how **AI Use Cases** is organized so you can find what you need fast — and so the library can grow without turning into a junk drawer.

## Two levels: domain and sub-domains

**AI Use Cases** is the whole domain. Inside it are **sub-domains** — each a self-contained collection aimed at a particular kind of user or setting. A sub-domain is the unit that gets added, maintained, and read on its own. Today there are four; more will follow.

- **[Everyday Tasks](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/index.md)** — for a non-technical person getting things done with an AI chat tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot). No code, no accounts to wire up, no jargon.
- **[Executive Leadership](/ai-use-cases/executive-leadership/index.md)** — AI recipes and workflows for the C-suite, organized by the leadership job in front of you: strategy, finance, people, board, communications, and decisions.
- **[Realtors](/ai-use-cases/realtors/index.md)** — paste-ready recipes for real-estate agents, organized by the job in front of you: listings and marketing, prospecting and outreach, and client communication.
- **[AI Playbooks](/ai-use-cases/ai-playbooks/index.md)** — procedures an AI assistant reads and runs on its own across your everyday tools, like a morning brief or inbox triage. Read-first and safe: it reads and drafts, but never sends or changes anything.

Future sub-domains (for example, deeper or more specialized cuts) will sit alongside these, each free to set its own depth and sourcing.

## Inside Everyday Tasks: organized by the task, not the tool

Everyday Tasks is grouped by **what you're trying to do** — the verb — rather than by job title or by app. You think "I need to *write* something" or "I need to *summarize* this," and go straight there. There are ten task types:

- **[Writing](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/writing/index.md)** — draft from a blank page
- **[Editing & Rewriting](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/editing-rewriting/index.md)** — improve text you already have
- **[Summarizing](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/summarizing/index.md)** — compress something long
- **[Explaining & Researching](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/explaining-researching/index.md)** — understand something fast
- **[Brainstorming & Ideation](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/brainstorming-ideation/index.md)** — generate options
- **[Planning](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/planning/index.md)** — turn intent into a plan
- **[Replying & Communicating](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/replying-communicating/index.md)** — handle messages and tricky conversations
- **[Deciding & Comparing](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/deciding-comparing/index.md)** — weigh options to choose
- **[Learning & Practicing](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/learning-practicing/index.md)** — study and rehearse
- **[Sorting & Structuring](/ai-use-cases/everyday-tasks/sorting-structuring/index.md)** — turn messy info into clean lists and tables

The sections aren't a sequence — you don't work through them in order. Jump to the one that matches your task.

## What a page gives you

Every page is a **recipe, not an essay**. You get a short setup, a **ready-to-paste prompt**, and a concrete **before → after** so you can see what good looks like. Copy the prompt, drop in your own details, and run it. That's the whole idea: you should leave each page having *done* the thing, not just read about it.
