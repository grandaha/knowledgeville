OKF is a young draft, so its future is a question, not a plan. This is our read of where it could go, held honestly against what it does not yet do.

## Where the format is heading

A few directions look plausible. Richer [provenance and verification](/open-knowledge-format/01-understanding-okf/03-provenance-and-trust.md) could become a norm, not just a citation habit. [Agent-native access](/open-knowledge-format/02-the-potential/01-knowledge-for-agents.md), through interfaces like MCP, could become a first-class way to distribute a bundle. Conventions for discovery and trust could emerge, alongside an ecosystem of tools and bundles that reaches beyond any single knowledge base.

## The gaps in v0.1 today

Credibility here depends on naming the gaps plainly. The format is young, and it shows.

Provenance is minimal. Today it is a citations convention, nothing more. There is no standardized verification or trust layer, so a reader cannot check a bundle's claims through the format itself.

There is no fixed concept taxonomy. That is a deliberate choice. It keeps authoring flexible, but it costs you consistency across bundles and makes discovery harder.

There is no standard for discovery or registry. You have no defined way to find bundles, or to know that a given bundle is authoritative. Versioning and governance are also early, as you would expect from a draft.

## What would have to be true

For the trajectory above to hold, several conditions would need to fall into place.

Provenance would have to grow past a convention into something a machine can check. That means a shared trust layer, agreed and adopted, not just proposed. Without it, "verified" stays a claim rather than a property.

Discovery would need a real standard, so bundles can be found and ranked by authority. Governance would have to mature enough to carry breaking changes without fracturing the ecosystem. The versioning plan already signals intent here: minor bumps for backward-compatible additions, major bumps for breaking changes. Adoption is the harder part. A format becomes a standard when tools and authors outside its origin choose to build on it.

## Our read

This is our view, not a forecast and not the maintainers' roadmap. We think the direction is sound. The gaps are the honest kind: known, named, and the sort a draft standard is expected to close over time. The open question is not the design. It is whether enough people build on it to make discovery, trust, and governance real rather than planned.
